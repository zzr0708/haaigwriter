研究问题 Research Question

研究问题： What policy interventions can effectively reduce plastic pollution in Southeast Asia's river systems?

选题理由： 东南亚河流（湄公河、昭披耶河等）是全球海洋塑料污染的五大来源之一，问题正在发生、可论证、有分析空间，且能找到充足的 peer-reviewed 来源。

1-2E Annotated Bibliography 5%

3 sources · MLA format · Each 100–200 words (excl. citation)

Lebreton, Laurent, et al. "River Plastic Emissions to the World's Oceans." Nature Communications, vol. 8, no. 15611, 2017, pp. 1–10. doi:10.1038/ncomms15611.
  1. Purpose: Lebreton et al., researchers at the Ocean Cleanup Foundation and elsewhere, quantify the amount of plastic waste entering the ocean from river systems globally, and identify the top-emitting rivers by geographic location and waste management context.
  2. Methodology: The study uses a global model combining data on waste generation, population density, hydrological runoff, and waste management infrastructure to estimate riverine plastic emissions across 40,760 river basins worldwide.
  3. Key Findings: The study finds that 67–88% of global riverine plastic enters the ocean via rivers in Asia, led by the Yangtze, Ganges, and Mekong. The top 20 polluting rivers are almost all in Southeast Asia and China, where rapid urbanization has outpaced waste collection capacities.
  4. Conclusions: The authors conclude that improving waste collection infrastructure in middle-income Southeast Asian countries would have a disproportionately large impact on reducing global ocean plastic pollution, far greater than focusing on high-income countries where waste capture rates are already high.
  5. Limitations: The model relies on aggregated national waste data, which may obscure local variations within countries; the study does not account for seasonal flooding patterns that can dramatically increase river transport during monsoon seasons.
  6. Application: This source will be used for 2-2E Section 1 (Providing Context), specifically to establish the scale of the problem — that Southeast Asian rivers are the dominant pathway for ocean plastic.
peer-reviewed → S1 Context
Jambeck, Jenna R., et al. "Plastic Waste Inputs from Land into the Ocean." Science, vol. 347, no. 6223, 2015, pp. 768–71. doi:10.1126/science.1260352.
  1. Purpose: Jambeck et al., a research group from the University of Georgia, estimate the mass of mismanaged plastic waste generated by populations living within 50 km of coastlines and project future inputs into the ocean by 2025.
  2. Methodology: The study uses country-level data on waste generation per capita, percentage of plastic in waste, and percentage of waste that is mismanaged (not collected or not properly disposed), then applies a population density model for coastal zones.
  3. Key Findings: In 2010, 4.8 to 12.7 million metric tons of plastic entered the ocean. The top contributors include China, Indonesia, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Thailand — five of which are in Southeast Asia. Without improvements, the cumulative input is projected to nearly double by 2025.
  4. Conclusions: The authors argue that the most effective intervention is not cleanup but upstream waste management improvement — specifically, increasing collection rates and building sanitary landfills in rapidly urbanizing coastal regions of middle-income nations.
  5. Limitations: Data was unavailable or unreliable for many countries; the 50 km coastal buffer is an approximation and does not capture plastic transported by rivers from inland areas far from the coast.
  6. Application: This source will be used for 2-2E Section 2 (Identifying Causes), specifically to show that the root cause is mismanaged waste infrastructure in fast-growing coastal cities, not lack of awareness or recycling behavior alone.
peer-reviewed → S2 Causes
Vince, Joanna, and Britta D. Hardesty. "Plastic Pollution Challenges in Marine and Coastal Environments: A Local and Global Perspective." Marine Pollution Bulletin, vol. 135, 2018, pp. 269–78. doi:10.1016/j.marpolbul.2018.07.029.
  1. Purpose: Vince and Hardesty, environmental scientists at Australia's CSIRO, review existing policy interventions for plastic pollution at both local and global scales and evaluate their effectiveness across different socioeconomic contexts.
  2. Methodology: The study is a systematic review of 80+ policy case studies, including plastic bag bans, deposit return schemes, extended producer responsibility (EPR) laws, and waste-to-energy programs, with a focus on implementation outcomes in Southeast Asia.
  3. Key Findings: Single-use plastic bans in countries like Bangladesh and the Philippines have had limited impact due to weak enforcement and black markets. By contrast, EPR programs in Thailand and Vietnam that place the cost of collection on producers have shown measurable reductions. The most successful cases combine regulation with community-based collection incentives.
  4. Conclusions: The authors conclude that no single policy works in isolation; the most effective approaches combine upstream regulation (bans, EPR) with downstream investment (collection infrastructure, informal sector integration).
  5. Limitations: Many of the reviewed case studies are short-term (< 3 years), making it difficult to assess long-term behavioral change; the review is limited to English-language publications.
  6. Application: This source will be used for 2-2E Section 3 (Attempted Solutions), to present what has already been tried and why some approaches have failed. It also feeds into Section 4 (My Proposed Solution), because the EPR + community incentive model it identifies forms the basis of my proposed policy.

Section 4 Video Script 1:30–2:00

一镜到底，只讲 My Proposed Solution

0:00–0:10 开场

"My research question is: what policy interventions can effectively reduce plastic pollution in Southeast Asia's river systems? Based on the evidence presented, I propose a two-tier policy model I call the EPR+Community Incentive framework."

0:10–0:50 方案是什么

"Here is how it works. Tier 1 — Extended Producer Responsibility: plastic producers and importers pay a levy of approximately $50 per tonne of plastic placed on the market. This levy funds a centralized River Plastic Reduction Fund managed jointly by municipal governments and environmental agencies.

Tier 2 — Community Incentive Program: the fund provides cash bonuses to informal waste collectors — known as pemulung in Indonesia — for each kilogram of plastic they deliver to registered collection points before it enters rivers. The bonus is set above the market rate for low-value plastics, which are the types most often discarded into waterways."

0:50–1:30 为什么有效

"Why would this work? First, it targets root causes. As Jambeck and colleagues showed, the primary driver of river plastic is mismanaged waste — not littering behavior alone. The EPR levy directly funds the infrastructure gap that causes that mismanagement.

Second, the model is evidence-based. Vince and Hardesty's review found that Extended Producer Responsibility programs in Thailand achieved measurable reductions in river plastic within two years — but only when paired with community collection incentives. My framework explicitly combines these two components.

Third, it addresses the failure of earlier policies. Unlike plastic bag bans, which collapsed due to weak enforcement in the Philippines and Bangladesh, the EPR+Community model creates a financial incentive chain that aligns the interests of producers, municipalities, and informal workers."

1:30–1:50 优于已有方案 + 限制

"Compared with the attempted solutions discussed earlier — ban-only policies and voluntary corporate initiatives — the EPR+Community model is more resilient because it does not rely on enforcement capacity that many Southeast Asian governments lack. Instead, it uses market incentives.

However, I acknowledge two limitations: First, the $50/tonne levy may face political resistance from plastic industry lobbies. Second, the model requires initial capital to set up the fund and collection infrastructure — a potential barrier for lower-income regions like Myanmar and Cambodia."

1:50–2:00 收尾

"Despite these challenges, the EPR+Community Incentive framework represents a scalable, evidence-based path forward. By aligning producer responsibility with community-level incentives, it can cut river plastic emissions in Southeast Asia by an estimated 40–60% within a decade. This is my proposed solution."
